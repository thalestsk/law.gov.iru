History of Imperial Royal Union

Mercantile Aristocracy

Coastal Merchant Settlements

Much of maritime IRU was settled by refugees escaping the oppression and serfdom from the Kingdom of Pelogrinne. These settlers established thriving port cities and trading hubs with sole focus of wealth accumulation. As the wealth grew, so did the influence of the merchant class. From time to time, wealthy aristocrats from Pelogrinne seeking opportunities began to settle in these coastal cities, bringing their own influence and resources. This occurred in tandem with a period of market liberalisation in Pelogrinne, that built strong trade links between the outer provinces of Pelogrinne and the coastal free cities.

The Pelogrinnean Threat

Early settlers were cautious of Pelogrinnean ambitions and had settled far from the outer provinces in hopes of maintaining distance from the Kingdom of Pelogrinne. As the otuer provinces of Pelogrinne expanded outwards, this distance gradually disappeared. This source of tension was exacerbated by the relative wealth of the coastal free cities, in which the wealth of the outer provinces was slowly but surely gravitating towards, and with it, the political influence over the outer provinces.

The Sydonis Confederation

The Sydonis Confederation was a loose confederation of the most prosperous coastal cities, formed to coordinate defense and trade policies against a potential Pelogrinnean threat, instead of investing into a sizeable army, these cities sought to fortify their settlements that would dissuade any potential invaders. This confederation slowly grew to include wealthier settlements in the outer provinces where the local aristocrats had put together a well-funded military. The loose confederation could no longer be tolerated by the Kingdom of Pelogrinne, which sought to bring these territories under its control, which viewed it as an attempt to secede. In 1433 CE, the Pelogrinnean army steamrolled through its outer provinces, conquering them and bringing them under direct control. The ruling aristocrats fled to the fortified coastal strongholds, bringing with them their armies and wealth. Having achieved the deterrent effect, the Kingdom of Pelogrinne reconsidered its attempts to conquer the coastal strongholds, as it was well aware that any attempt to do so would result in a prolonged and costly war. Meanwhile, the political resolve of the confederation to collective defence resulted in the founding proclamation of the State of Common Good, for the primary purpose of entrusting a sovereign of higher standing to the King of Pelogrinne to protect what the confederation had built. And so, the State of Common Good was born as an empire in 1468 CE, purely for the reason that the Emperor was of a higher standing than the King.

Early Empire

The Imperial Royal Union

The IRU was an empire in name only, but functionally a union of the aristocracy in the southern coastal trading settlement of Elstreese and the former exiled pretender to the Pelogrinnean throne, who had been implicated in a made-up conspiracy to assassinate the King, who had the political backing of the merchant elite in Sydonis. Yet they had chosen as sovereign the architect of the defences of Sydonis, who was no more than a commoner well-versed in military strategy.

Pelogrinnean Restlessness

Having a realised secessionist movement that in name subverted the King of Pelogrinne right on its borders, the Kingdom of Pelogrinne resolved itself to conquering the Empire. In order to do so, it began developing coastal trading towns in its limited coastal regions in an attempt to capture the wealth that had been flowing towards the IRU. But alas, a continental power like the Kingdom of Pelogrinne soon found itself embargoed by the merchant class of the IRU, which had the naval power and political influence over foreign merchants to enforce such a policy.

First Pelogrinnean War

The embargo produced a severe economic crisis in both IRU and the Kingdom of Pelogrinne, which forced a premature war in 1502 CE unintended by both sides, where the IRU political calculus had not underestimated the Pelogrinnean overreaction. This war stalemated from the outset, with neither side able to gain a decisive advantage. In 1509 CE, both sides agreed to a peace. Introspection on the part of the IRU forced the merchant elite to relinquish control over economic policy to the sovereign, who by a marriage to the former pretender to the Pelogrinnean throne, had established the Crown of IRU, held by the newly-crowned Emperor Thales I, in direct opposition to the Crown of Pelogrinne.

Second Pelogrinnean War

When King Maximilian VI of Pelogrinne died in 1516 CE, his son and heir, King Maximilian VII, would not stand for the insult dealt to this father in the establishment of the Crown of IRU. He declared war on the IRU in 1517 CE, seeking to quash the seemingly irreversible secession. Instead of a direct fight, Emperor Thales I maintained a defensive posture, allowing the Pelogrinnean forces to advance but not consolidate their gains. An invasion fleet assembled from the merchant marine captured the coastal town of Beruma, which imposed a humiliating defeat for King Maximilian VII in 1523 CE. Upon his abdication in favour of his son, Beruma was returned to Pelogrinne in exchange for some of the outer provinces of Pelogrinne to form a sufficient buffer zone between both sides.

The Rise of the Empire

The Third Pelogrinnean War

Emperor Thales II presided over a period of relative stability and prosperity. The newly acquired provinces were integrated into the imperial administration, leading to stable economic development that directly funded the army and navy. His heir, Emperor Thales III, recognised that the continental hinterland remained open to invasion and was never properly secured. In 1590 CE, Emperor Thales III declared war against Pelogrinne in a pre-emptive strike to force territorial concessions before Pelogrinne could prepare for an invasion. This proved successful and resulting in cessations territory up to the Chalaronne river in the north and Esselles river in the south.

The Bureaucratic Reforms of 1614

The new territorial concessions posed a bigger threat to the security of the IRU as the border with Pelogrinne now quadrupled in length, making it much more difficult to defend. Emperor Thales IV, who succeeded his father, attributed this critical error to the aristocratic class that made the bureaucracy of the state, who had failed to put together a coherent diplomatic policy. In 1614 CE, the beginnings of IRU's modern system of governance began to take shape with the ousting of the aristocracy and the establishment of a more merit-based administration, and with concentration of political power in the Emperor.

The Fourth Pelogrinnean War

Seeing the inherent weakness formed by the territorial composition of the IRU, Pelogrinne launched a full-scale invasion in 1620 CE, seeking to reclaim lost territories and establish a buffer zone. The IRU responded with a unified military effort, mobilising its professional army and navy to defend the homeland. Initial gains by both sides quickly eroded into a prolonged stalemate, with neither side able to achieve a decisive victory. Peace ensued in 1646 CE.

Interbellum

Despite the uneasy peace, war fatigue was widespread across both nations. Emperors Thales IV, Thales V and Thales VI all focused on reconstruction, domestic reforms and diplomacy to avert war. This period of unprecedented stability and growth cemented the centrality of the monarchy in the IRU. In 1734 CE, Emperor Thales VII ascended to the throne and implemented the most comprehensive reforms in the IRU's history, organising the lands into Cantons and instituted the princes of the royal family as the lords of each Canton, in an attempt to delegate administrative responsibilities and strengthen central control.

The Falling Dominoes from the Waldronian Crisis

Instead of fighting the IRU, the Kingdom of Pelogrinne opted to declare war on the Kingdom of Waldronia in the south, seeking to cut off the IRU from one of its key trading partners, a strategy that would prove successful. The decades of prosperity had reduced the appetite for war, and Emperor Aldrin I, who had succeeded Emperor Thales VII in 1760 CE, was reluctant to engage in another costly conflict. This encouraged the Kingdom of Pelogrinne, which made newfound gains against the Duchy of Alephescia and Principality of Altessa in separate conquests in 1766 CE and 1778 CE respectively.

Build-Up to War

Empress Rothsanna I presided over a fractuous period of unease. The army had not experienced a war over a century, and complacency was abound in the military establishment. Fear surrounding the growing power of Pelogrinne and the weakening of Waldronia led to a growing consensus that the IRU needed to prepare for war. When Emperor Thales VIII succeeded the throne in 1805 CE, he inherited a rejuvenated military and a strengthened navy, ready to face the growing threat of Pelogrinne. Specifically, he had inherited a small, professional, well-equipped and highly adaptive force that had speed as its main advantage.

Fifth Pelogrinnean War

Not wanting to let his advantage erode, Emperor Thales VIII launched a surprise war against Pelogrinne in 1806 CE. Diplomatic efforts secured the cooperation of the Alephescians, Altessans and Waldronians, who declared war on Pelogrinne and kept the Pelogrinnean forces occupied on multiple fronts. On the IRU front, the war was a resounding success. Pelogrinnean forces were routinely routed by 1812 CE, the front reached the Pelogrinnean heartlands. Instead of facing the IRU in a direct conflict, the main armies of Pelogrinne focused on decisively defeating and subjugating the smaller states, so that the might of Pelogrinne can be brought to bear entirely on the IRU. Emperor Thales VIII, however, saw the window of opportunity closing. The peace of 1813 CE effectively recognised Pelogrinnean control over Eastern Asterrania, and IRU control over Western Asterrania. Yet, the IRU was in a much more secure position, as border was within a couple of weeks' reach from the Pelogrinnean capital. This, however, was viewed by the smaller states as a betrayal.

The Second Empire

Treaty of Triesenberg

Emperor Thales IX spent much of his reign repairing the diplomatic standing caused by the fallout of the Fifth Pelogrinnean War. Emerging as the sole victor with territorial concessions severely damaged the national prestige. His conciliatory approach was not in vain. The Treaty of Triesenberg in 1850 CE, veiled behind the threat of war, forced the Kingdom of Pelogrinne to release Alephescia, Altessa and Waldronia as independent states, in exchange for the permanent recognition of the present-day borders.

Pelogrinnean Revolution

The Pelogrinnean Revolution immediately followed the Treaty of Trisenberg. Faced with a total collapse of national prestige, the Republic of Pelogrinne was established after the King of Pelogrinne was deposed in the Pelogrinnean Revolution of 1850 CE. This had a ripple effect in nationalist republican movements across the continent.

The Princely Problem

When Empress Rothsanna II assumed power in 1853 CE, the Crown found itself in an untenable position. The Crown had a princely problem; there were princes with superior administrative experience and foresight, developing their Cantons in new and innovative ways, finding growth in the unlikeliest of places; there were also princes who were plainly indifferent and had no interest in the business of government, often neglecting their duties and leaving their Cantons to their own devices; and worst of all, there were princes who treated the Crown and its subjects with utter contempt, openly defying the Empress and instituting brutal regimes that oppressed their populations. This gave fuel to the growing republican movement brewing within the Cantons dissastisfied with their princes.

The Solution

The Crown's solution was an elegant compromise at the national level, that it would withdraw its involvement in the Canton's business, and instead delegate the responsibility to the Cantons themselves, and limit its scope on national matters to only when it was consulted by the democratically-elected national government. The Crown refused to institutionalise the reforms, insisting it would have needed to be a matter of custom and precedent, rather than law. In her observation of other republics, Empress Rothsanna II noted that the power-hungry nature of the republican movement might eventually work in the favour of the Crown, and that the Crown could no longer be a convenient target for the republican movement, and the ensuing infighting might implode the movement. As for the princes, the performing ones stayed on to guide the makings of the national government while the rest opted to retire when the Crown withdrew its protection overnight. Symbolically, the democratisation is commemorated at the founding of the Second Empire.

Democracy and the Modern IRU

Gradual Recession of the Monarchy

After Empress Rothsanna II, the short-lived reigns of Emperors Thales X and Thales XI did no favours to the Crown's reputation, and the monarchy gradually receded into the background, to the point that the democratically-elected government refused to consult with it anymore. However, in doing so, the government also severed its training wheels. What followed was an immediate spiral for further devolution of power - the royalist Cantons refused the new order, as they were beneficiaries of the Crown's benevolent rule, while the republican Cantons sought to appropriate the wealth of the royalist Cantons for their own economic development. When control of the national government oscillated between both factions, they would be frustrated at every level by their counterparts in the Cantons.

Reduced to Ceremonial Duties

Emperors Aldrin II and Thales XII both saw their roles diminished to mere ceremonial duties, although they had observed correctly that the population generally welcomed their presence as a constant in an everchanging world. Emperor Thales XII, despite the benevolence and lively outreach that connected with the common folk, was beset with ill health. Where the population sought respite in the monarch's presence, that was only temporary relief from the chaos of the times. No national government since 1997 had finished a full term in office. Between 2009 and 2012, there were 3 elections at the national level, producing a total of 5 different governments formed by an odd association of the most unexpected combinations of political factions.

The July Regency

Political appetite for reform was at a peak by June 2012. On 4 July 2012, the sudden but not unexpected death of Emperor Thales XII shocked the nation, and Princess Rothsanna assumed the role of Regent over Emperor Thales XIII, who was only 15 years old at the time of succession. Princess Rothsanna used her position as Regent and colluded in secret with royalist and republican elements of the former national governments embedded within the security services. Having successfully misled the armed forces into supporting their cause, Princess Rothsanna launched her coup to seize control from the democratically-elected government of the day in the name of the Emperor. This was met with outrage across the entire political spectrum. Critically, to the ordinary citizen, having lived through chaos and currently mourning the loss of the late Emperor Thales XII, simply could not allow such a blatant power grab to stand. Demonstrations erupted across the nation, with citizens taking to the streets in unprecedented numbers to demand the restoration of the rule of law.

The End of the Coup

The Supreme Court, in defiance of the Regent, met in secret with Emperor Thales XIII and other members of the royal family who were equally horrified at the turn of events. They had all concluded that if the Crown and the IRU as a nation was to survive, it would have to act decisively to restore the rule of law. On 14 July 2012, while the Supreme Court announced an emergency ruling on the legality of the coup, Emperor Thales XIII dispatched the household troops against the Regent. The sight of the Emperor appearing before the Supreme Court declaring the illegality of the coup immediately dispelled any notion that the Crown was complicit in the coup, and was an open declaration of the independence and authority of the Crown as the ultimate guarantor of the rule of law. In its emergency ruling, the Supreme Court reaffirmed the position of the Crown and ruled on the matter of law that only the Crown had the authority to intervene, and that the hardship and uncertainty imposed upon the nation was the effect of decades of sidelining the Crown's role in ensuring stability and continuity. To that effect, the restoration of order by the Crown must effectively be a restoration of the Crown's rightful place in the governance of the nation, going back to the first principles laid out in the founding proclamation.

The New Consensus

For the common folk, the Supreme Court's ruling had no real effect on their perception of the state of chaos. They had lived through decades of instability and were weary of political games. What mattered to them was stability, security, and the rule of law. The Supreme Court's ruling was a legal technicality that they did not fully understand. They could not care for who was, formed, or represented the government, for as long as a functional government existed. This was also the political consensus that the moderate elements of the political spectrum had reached - that the role of the Crown was to ensure stability and continuity, and that their legitimacy derived directly from the proper functioning of the Crown itself. This coalesced into the National Security Coalition as the grassroots movement in support of stability and the common understanding to set aside political differences for pragmatism.

The Existential Question

The hardcore republican question — what happens when the Emperor is not a stabilising figure — appears to demand a constitutional answer. It does not. It demands a constitutional performance that makes the answer permanently unnecessary.

This is because the question is existentially destructive regardless of how it is answered. The only viable answer is no answer — and everyone sophisticated enough to matter understands this implicitly.

But it cannot solely fall to the Emperor to be the stabilising figure; the political elite of the day needs to be equally committed to stability and equally invested in the common prosperity, borne out of wisdom and strength, for the constancy of consensus. Accordingly only correct interpretation of the existential question is as follows:

The consensus is most stable when nobody tests it. It is most legitimate when nobody needs to invoke its ultimate authority. It works best when the question it defers never needs to be asked. And it survives longest when every actor understands that asking the question destroys the answer.

The consensus is therefore not a compromise between positions — it is a shared recognition that some questions are more dangerous than their answers are valuable. The long-term constitutional question will never be answered — not because it cannot be, but because everyone who matters has independently concluded that the asking of it is the answer to nothing and the destruction of everything.

The Final Character of the Constitutional Order

A constitutional order built on the memory of catastrophe, sustained by performance, protected by the deliberate non-answering of its own existential question, governed through layered democratic institutions at the cantonal level where life is actually lived, overseen by a Crown whose legitimacy has been earned rather than merely inherited, and held together by a coalition of pragmatists who understand that stability is not the absence of disagreement but the management of disagreement within a framework that everyone values more than they value winning.

This is the shared wisdom that the alternative was experienced, survived, and must never be revisited.

As the founding proclamation defines as the objective:

"pour la sagesse, la force, la constance et la prospérité du Domaine."

"for the wisdom, strength, constancy, and prosperity of the Domain."
