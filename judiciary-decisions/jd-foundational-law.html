Judiciary Decisions -
Foundational Law

The list of standing interpretations of law upheld by the Supreme Court of Judicature on matters of foundational law relating to the Proclamation and the interpretive Edicts.

Fluidity of the Common Good

Lindis v. The State, 1868 - The Court declines to affix a settled definition to the Common Good, for it is not a term ordained to stand in fixed meaning, but one whose force lies in its capacity to answer to circumstance. To define it in the abstract would be to confine that which must remain responsive, and to substitute rigidity where judgment is required. As a thing not yet called into final form admits of more than one state, so too the Common Good may bear differing yet lawful expressions until the moment of necessity compels its application. It is therefore the duty of the Court not to pronounce upon it beyond what the present cause requires, but only to determine whether the measure in question may be sustained within a coherent understanding of the State’s ordering.

Re The Triesenberg Daily, 2025 - The Court does not undertake to define the Common Good, for it is not a term to be confined by fixed meaning, but one realised in its application. The Court is not concerned with the content of the Common Good in the abstract.

The Common Good as a Shield

Re The Triesenberg Daily, 2025 - The Court finds that where a public authority permits ambiguity to persist, and declines to give timely account where it is able, it cannot thereafter rely upon the Common Good to shield itself from the consequences of that ambiguity. It is sufficient to state that its invocation does not excuse conduct which, by ordinary prudence, ought to have been clarified or resolved. The Court rejects its improper use as a substitute for clarity, candour, and the reasonable expectations of those subject to its exercise.

Preservation of the Unity of Law

Re Fouché, 1961 - The Court proceeds upon the principle that the law within the Domain is one and indivisible, notwithstanding its application across differing places and circumstances. Where divergence arises between courts, it is the duty of the judicial system to restore coherence, so that no conflicting constructions are permitted to stand in parallel. The Court shall therefore act, where necessary, to reconcile such differences, not for the sake of uniformity alone, but to preserve the unity of authority from which all law proceeds.

Necessity of Escalation

Re Fouché, 1961 - The Court recognises that not every cause warrants advancement beyond the forum in which it is first determined, and that the progression of matters through the courts must be governed by necessity rather than by right. Accordingly, a cause shall be admitted to a higher court only where its determination bears upon the coherence of law, the reconciliation of conflicting judgments, or the proper ordering of authority within the State. It is not the purpose of escalation to secure repeated adjudication, but to ensure that the law remains consistent and intelligible across the Domain.

Role of the Supreme Court of Judicature

Re Fouché, 1961 - The Court affirms that its jurisdiction is confined to matters that engage the Proclamation, the Edicts, or the unity of law derived therefrom, and shall not extend to the general correction of error in individual causes. It shall not entertain any matter that does not, in substance, raise a question of foundational authority or of such divergence as affects the coherence of the State's law. In exercising its powers, the Court shall act with restraint, intervening only where necessary to preserve the integrity of the legal order, and not as a court of ordinary appeal.

Presumption of Ordinary Cause

The State v. Wahlgren, 1912 - The Court proceeds upon the presumption that conduct, whether of persons or of the State, arises from ordinary cause—being error, limitation, or the press of circumstance—rather than from design or malice, unless the contrary is plainly shown. To impute intention without sufficient proof is to depart from reasoned judgment and to substitute conjecture for evidence. It is therefore incumbent upon the party alleging deliberate wrongdoing to establish the same with clarity and substance; failing which, the Court shall prefer the more probable and commonplace explanation, and determine the cause accordingly.

Reality of Human Conduct

Bartolo v. Simms, 1863 - The Court recognises that in the affairs of men, conduct is most commonly shaped by fear and self-interest, and that the application of law must proceed with due regard to this ordinary condition. It is neither the function nor the presumption of the Court to rely upon idealised notions of behaviour, but to assess actions as they arise in the real circumstances of life. In so doing, the Court shall interpret evidence, intention, and consequence with an understanding that persons act to preserve themselves and to advance their interests, and shall frame its judgment accordingly, so that the law remains grounded in reality and not in abstraction.

Permissibility of Differentiation

Winter v. Atwood Assurance, 2011 - The Court affirms that the State is constituted upon a singular and indivisible conception of the Common Good, and that measures which fracture that unity are impermissible; however, it departs from the reasoning of the court below, which erred in treating the absence of individual choice and the scale of the affected class as determinative, and in doing so ventured beyond interpretation into the making of policy. The absence of choice is not sufficient to establish incoherence, as governance necessarily responds to unchosen conditions, nor does population proportion provide a principled basis for validity. The proper inquiry is whether the impugned measure effects a categorical and enduring partition of the polity, assigning systematically different baseline burdens or benefits to fixed classes independent of individual conduct, such that the Common Good is no longer applied as a unified standard but as distinct and non-reconcilable variants. Differentiation that is continuous, dynamic, or functional—including adjustments reflecting conditions through which persons may pass or mechanisms sustaining a common system—does not, without more, fracture that unity, whereas classification on a fixed and categorical basis that permanently situates individuals within distinct classes bearing systematically different relations to the Common Good does so unless expressly and coherently authorised by the State.
